TIME TO COLLECT  September 14, 2023

Time to Collect is a game made in UE 5.3 by a team I put together consisting of some programmer and artist friends of mine.

You play as a rat who collects objects scattered around the room while avoiding a deadly roomba and other security, take a look!

VERSION CONTROL

Our game is built on Unreal Engine 5 but we opted to use Unity's Plastic SCM as our source control. Setting up Plastic was a bit of hurdle in the beginning, but once we set up the right ignore.config it was smooth sailing.


FEATURES: Procedural Room Generation

I love random elements in games and Proc Gen is one of my favorite things to implement. It was the feature I spent the most time getting just right.

I used a grid based system and applied some principles of grid traversal. The grid was populated with numbers representing empty or occupied spaces or coordinates where an object is to be spawned. Then, I iterate through the grid again, spawning in objects and check against the surrounding grid tiles to determine how I need to orient the object.

To prevent cases where a bed would spawn right in front of a door or a dresser would spawn with no room for a person to access it, I would mark tiles as "occupied" wherever a piece of furniture would logically have empty space around it. All furnitures were placed against the wall, so this also required checks on the surrounding tiles to determine the orientation.




FEATURES: Movement

One of my goals for this game was to do my part of the work in C++ whenever possible. Unreal's blueprint system is powerful, but I enjoy the familiarity of C++ as well as the ease of readability.

The movement of the Rat is code I adapted from an earlier exercise project. It additionally implements a charge-jump which allows the Rat to gain greater jump height the longer you hold Spacebar. I worked with our artists to integrate character movement with animations, since I had prior experience with this in another project.


FEATURES: Wordle

Inside Time to Collect is my implementation of Wordle. It contains the entire list of possible 5-letter solutions used in NYT's Wordle, as well as their list of possible guesses.

The Wordle logic was simple, but before I could work on the logic, I needed to figure out how to programmatically create a grid to hold the guesses. After much searching, I finally discovered WidgetTrees and how to use them. As always, the solution is simple and intuitive in hindsight.
